Cross-Asset Momentum Strategy

Spring 2026
In Progress

Rank-weighted momentum strategy across 14 ETFs spanning commodities, equity sectors, fixed income, and currencies. Uses trailing 12-month returns with an EMA trend confirmation filter. Three-tier signal weighting (STRONG/NORMAL/WEAKENING as 1.3x/1.0x/0.5x rank multipliers), 25% single-asset concentration cap, 2% turnover buffer, and a graduated macro regime filter that scales exposure based on safe-haven asset signals.

Backtested over 14 years with a Sharpe ratio of 0.78 and +80% cumulative momentum spread. Currently running a 4-week live test on Interactive Brokers with $50M simulated capital for a graduate-level quantitative investment course at Duke. Iterated through eight design versions after critique that fixed weekly rebalancing was incompatible with momentum dynamics.

Python (pandas, NumPy, yfinance), Interactive Brokers TWS/API

Econometric Modelling & Risk Analysis

Fall 2025
Completed

Analyzed JPMorgan Chase equity returns against the DJIA index (June 2017 - June 2023). Estimated market beta (~1.2) through linear regression with Breusch-Pagan and Jarque-Bera diagnostics for heteroscedasticity and normality, comparing full-period vs pre-COVID results to identify structural shifts.

Selected ARMA(4,5) via AIC grid search with Ljung-Box residual validation. GARCH(1,1) via BIC search for volatility clustering. Extended into a VAR(9) framework across JPM, Goldman Sachs, American Express, and Visa to analyze cross-asset shock transmission via impulse response and Granger causality.

Python (statsmodels, pandas, NumPy)

When Genius Failed

on Roger Lowenstein's account of Long-Term Capital Management

When Genius Failed is a brilliant account of the rise and downfall of one of the most elusive hedge funds on Wall Street, Long-Term Capital Management. The man behind the firm, John Meriweather, was a former Salomon Brothers trader who ran the legendary bond desk. After what felt almost like a betrayal from one of his own team members led to his hasty resignation in 1991, he seemed to avenge it by dreaming up a new firm of his own. What followed was LTCM, a hedge fund that would be the talk of Wall Street for years to come and would count some of the biggest names in finance among its ranks.

The fathers of modern finance and economics, Myron Scholes and Robert Merton, both future Nobel laureates, along with a former Federal Reserve vice chairman and PhDs from the top institutions in the country, joined this dream team.

What followed was almost a domination of the Street. Conflated with their degrees and massive egos, this team believed they could do nothing wrong. To their credit, they did beat the markets and delivered strong returns for years. But as time passed, the greed grew without end. The leverage was enormous, the risk measures were not enough, and there was no one willing to say no. Meriweather, for all his skill as a trader, was not the strongest boss.

Eventually the positions they had built could not be unwound. LTCM ended up entangling many major firms in complex trades that would cost billions in losses, and the fund's capital simply wasn't enough to cover the risk across all its positions. The firm was rescued, in 1998, through a Fed-orchestrated bailout, but the story feels like Icarus flying too close to the sun. They were firm in their beliefs, unable to stay humble, work as a team, or hold to discipline in their trades.

They thought they could not lose, but what goes up must come down, like the wheel of fortune, and more relevantly, trading demands discipline, humility, and a clear understanding of the difference between theoretical research and actual markets, which eat their own all the time. The law of averages holds especially true in capital markets. The firm became a cautionary tale, and yet an intriguing read, one where the drama kept me hooked while painting a vivid picture of the attitude across Wall Street during a bull market that would eventually, and inevitably, reverse.
